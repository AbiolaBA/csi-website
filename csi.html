Methodology · v0.3 (Tier 1) + v0.4 (Tiers 2–3)

The Consumption Sovereignty Index

For each country and each year, the CSI measures how far a nation's consumption is supplied by its own productive economy, rather than by imports of goods it could have produced itself.

Conceptual foundation

Four ideas underpin the index.

Consumer sovereignty. In a market economy the consumer is the final authority — consumers "hire and fire" producers through what they choose to buy. If the consumer is sovereign, a country's aggregate consumption pattern is a decision, and decisions can be well or poorly aligned with the nation's long-run economic interest.

Consumer xenocentrism. The documented tendency, strongest in many developing economies, to prefer foreign goods because they are foreign — foreign origin functioning as a status signal. The CSI is not an attitude scale; it measures the economic footprint that value systems leave in trade data.

The CES microfoundation. Household consumption is a CES bundle of home and foreign goods (the Galí–Monacelli small-open-economy framework). The cost-minimising allocation implies that the observed foreign expenditure share is

s_F = α · (P_F / P)^(1 − η)

The observed foreign share confounds two distinct things: the structural preference α, and relative prices. A country can buy heavily foreign because foreign goods are cheaper or it genuinely cannot make them — or because of a cultural value system. The CSI is, formally, an estimate of (1 − α): the foreign-goods preference stripped of the part explained by prices and by genuine capability.

The capability–consumption gap. Exports reveal capability — a country cannot competitively export what it cannot competitively make. Imports reveal dependence. The CSI combines the two, and the index is the gap between them.

On the name. "Sovereignty" ties the index to consumer sovereignty — the idea that the consumer is the final authority in the economy — and relocates the measure from the person to the pattern. A country scores low because of its economic structure, never because its citizens are "less intelligent."

The method, step by step

Computed independently for each year, on Harvard Growth Lab Atlas country-product-year data at the 4-digit level (HS 1992 from 1995; SITC Rev. 2 from 1962).

1

Capability — PCI-aware

Could this country make product p? Capability is the maximum of two signals: product-space density (closeness of p to what the country already exports) and simplicity (one minus the percentile rank of product complexity).

density_cp = 1 − distance_cp simplicity_p = 1 − percentile_rank(PCI_p) cap_cp = max(density_cp, simplicity_p)

The max is what makes the index work: a basic consumer good — furniture, processed food, simple apparel — is within anyone's capability, while advanced electronics and pharmaceuticals still require revealed proximity.

2

Restrict to final consumption goods

Only final consumption goods are kept — about 370 of 1,242 HS headings (roughly 27–28% of world merchandise import value), assigned by a transparent, rule-based end-use classification following UN BEC Rev.5 logic, with chapter-level defaults and heading-level overrides. The full classification is published for review.

3

Import dependence and basket weights

dep_cp = max(0, (m_cp − x_cp) / (m_cp + x_cp)) w_cp = m_cp / Σ_P m_cp′

The net-import ratio is 0 under ordinary two-way trade and approaches 1 when the country only imports the product. Weights are shares of the consumption-good import bill.

4

The capability–consumption gap

g_cp = cap_cp · dep_cp CCG_c = Σ_P w_cp · g_cp ∈ [0, 1]

High when a country spends its consumer-import budget on goods it could make and genuinely depends on imports for. CCG is the Tier-1 estimate of the CES preference α.

5

The index

CSI_raw = 1 − CCG high = sovereign CSI = within-year z-score primary statistic CSI_100 = within-year percentile 0–100 presentation scale

The measurement ladder

The CSI targets the CES preference α. How completely it can be isolated depends on the data brought to bear:

Tier 1

Trade data only — the headline CSI. Adjusts for capability but not relative prices. Immediately available for 193 economies back to 1962.

Tier 2

+ domestic production. With UNIDO INDSTAT industrial production, the true foreign expenditure share s_F = imports / apparent consumption is measured directly, industry by industry, via an HS↔ISIC concordance.

Tier 3

+ relative prices. With price indices and an assumed elasticity η, the structural α is backed out of s_F by removing the price effect — the fully CES-consistent measure.

Tiers 2 and 3 are computed for 107 economies. Their near-perfect mutual correlation (+0.998) and near-zero correlation with Tier 1 (+0.06) is itself a finding: capability-mismatch and consumption-openness are different dimensions of sovereignty — which is why the lab publishes them as a family, not a single number.

Verification

Versions 0.1–0.2 defined the gap over all merchandise with capability as density alone. Verification found the index inverted: it correlated −0.39 with economic complexity and ranked petro-states at the top. The cause was structural, and v0.3 fixed it — restricting to final consumption goods and making capability complexity-aware. That failure and its diagnosis are kept in the methodology deliberately: the index's current shape is a result of what verification found.

  1. It no longer inversely tracks complexity. CSI × ECI correlation (HS92, 2023): +0.74, against −0.39 for the retired all-merchandise variant.
  2. Petro-states no longer dominate the top. Equatorial Guinea, second in the broken variant, now sits near the bottom.
  3. Independent classifications agree. HS92 and SITC rankings correlate at 0.975 in 2023 — the ranking is not an artefact of one product scheme.

The corrected ranking is economically coherent — and the product diagnostics behave as intended: Nigeria's gap is driven by imported sugar, frozen fish, apparel, footwear, motorcycles and, most pointedly, palm oil; imported pharmaceuticals appear only at low weight, because the index correctly treats medicines as genuinely complex.

Known limitations — stated plainly

  1. Underdevelopment vs the value system. Tier 1 cannot fully separate a country that imports consumer goods because of a value system from one that has not yet industrialised. The fix in progress is the peer-adjusted CSI: the residual after regressing on income and population, isolating the value-system signal from the development effect.
  2. No domestic production data at Tier 1. The net-import ratio proxies true import penetration; Tier 2 addresses this.
  3. No prices at Tier 1. CCG conflates α with the price effect; Tier 3 addresses this.
  4. Capability is inferred from density and complexity, not observed.
  5. Goods only — services are excluded.
  6. The consumption classification is an in-project construction following BEC logic, published in full for review and replaceable by the official UNSD correspondence.

Honesty note. The lab's critical review (June 2026) is published alongside the methodology — including the finding that Tier 1 and Tiers 2–3 measure different constructs, and that genuine α-identification needs PPP-decomposed consumption prices. Publishing our own strongest critique is the standard we want the field to hold us to.
