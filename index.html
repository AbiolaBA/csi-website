The Consumption Sovereignty Index — flagship measure

What a nation consumes is a structural lever — not only what it produces.

The Consumer Intelligence Lab builds indexes, open data and reproducible analysis of the consumption side of development. Our flagship measure, the Consumption Sovereignty Index (CSI), reads a country's import basket and asks a simple question: does this nation supply its own consumers, or import what it could have made itself?

A portrait of consumption sovereignty

What the index is really about

Behind every score is a real economy — the markets, farms and factories that decide whether a nation supplies its own consumers.

The idea

The import-side mirror of economic complexity

The Economic Complexity Index reads a country's export basket to infer what it can produce. The CSI reads its import basket of final consumer goods and asks: of the things this country's households buy from abroad, how much sits within the country's own productive capability?

A high CSI means consumption is sovereign — the nation largely supplies its own consumers. A low CSI means consumption leans on foreign supply of goods the country could make itself: the macroeconomic footprint of what the consumer-behaviour literature calls consumer xenocentrism.

The CSI is a diagnostic instrument, not a policy prescription. It measures a gap — and a gap can be closed by raising domestic quality and shifting value systems just as much as by any trade measure.

The index family

One measurement ladder, four dimensions

Each index isolates a different slice of the consumption decision. Together they climb toward the structural preference parameter α — the foreign-goods weight in the household's consumption bundle.

CSI — Capability

The headline index. Penalises importing final consumer goods the country demonstrably could make — capability from the export side, dependence from the import side. 193 economies, 1962–2024.

Findings

Not a wealth ranking

The top of the CSI ranking — Hungary, Denmark, Germany, Czechia, Spain — is dominated by diversified economies that supply their own consumers. But the index is not income in disguise: its correlation with GDP per capita is just +0.02 on the analysis panel.

Türkiye, Mexico, Thailand and Malaysia — middle-income manufacturers — rank above Japan and the United States, which are rich but import much of what their consumers buy. And the index pinpoints which goods drive each country's gap: for Nigeria, imported sugar, frozen fish, footwear — and palm oil, a product Nigeria once led the world in exporting.

At the ERSA Trade Policy Conference 2026 we showed the CSI is statistically associated with all three green-growth outcomes on the African subsample where two of three ECI variants fall silent — evidence that for African economies, the green-growth signal lives on the consumption side.

CSI-Preference: the within-good price-premium dimension

First results from BACI unit values overturn the simple xenocentrism story: premium-paying tracks income, not status — but the status signal survives, concentrated in culture-specific categories. Nigeria pays 56% above the world average for exactly one chapter: wigs and human hair.

Open science

Every number on this site is reproducible

The entire pipeline — from raw Atlas trade files to the ranking you can explore here — is a documented Python package driven by a single config file. A full refresh is one command:

# drop a new Atlas data vintage into data/raw/, then
python -m cii.run

Data vintages are recorded in a manifest; every parameter lives in config.yaml; the code is never edited to re-run. Download the package, the panels and the rankings from the data page.

For policy makers

A diagnostic for industrialisation strategy

For the Global South, the CSI turns an abstract debate about industrial policy into a concrete product list: the specific consumer goods a country buys abroad but has the demonstrated capability to make. That list is a targeting tool — for quality upgrading, for value-chain investment, and for the demand-side campaigns that keep income, and jobs, at home.

The lab's framing is deliberate: this is not import-substitution nostalgia. The gap can close through better domestic goods and changed value systems — the consumer stays sovereign throughout.
